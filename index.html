How this plan works

This is a modular curriculum — after finishing Modules 0–3 (the foundation), take the subject and filter modules in any order that matches the weather, the season, and your mood. Every module is sized for roughly one to two weeks at 3–5 hours: a short read, two or three field exercises, and one substantial assignment.

Three principles run through everything here:

  • The constraint is the teacher. The CS-Pro has no screen, no autofocus, no manual exposure. That removes every excuse to fiddle and forces the two skills that actually make photographs: seeing light and arranging the frame.
  • Commit, then review. You can't chimp. Shoot a full outing, then review on the computer like a contact sheet — this delayed feedback loop is the fastest way to calibrate your eye.
  • One variable at a time. Each exercise isolates a single idea (one kind of light, one compositional device, one filter). Mastery comes from repetition of small ideas, not from complicated shoots.

🏕️ Suggested path

0 → 1 → 2 → 3 (foundation, in order) → then alternate one subject module with one filter module: 4 → 8 → 5 → 9 → 6 → 7 → 10 → 11.

🌦️ Weather-driven path

Sunny week? Do 9 (red filter needs bright light). Overcast or foggy? 6 or 7. Golden evenings free? 1 and 4. Rain just ended? Reflections in 6.

📓 Keep a field log

The camera has no screen, so carry a pocket note: frame number, what you tried, what you predicted. Comparing predictions to results is where learning happens.

Part I — Foundation

MODULE 0

Meet your CS-Pro: thinking like the camera

~1 week · mostly indoors · gear: camera + USB-C cable

The Camp Snap CS-Pro is a screen-free 16 MP digital point-and-shoot with a rangefinder-inspired body: an optical viewfinder, a fixed wide-angle lens, a true xenon flash, and a four-position dial that switches between onboard film-style looks. Everything else — exposure, focus — is decided for you. Creative control therefore lives in exactly four places: where you stand, when you press, what light you choose, and what you put on the lens. The whole curriculum is built on those four levers.

The three quirks to internalize now

  • Parallax: the viewfinder sits above-left of the lens, so it doesn't see exactly what the lens sees — and the finder shows slightly less than the lens captures. Up close, aim a touch high-left of where the finder suggests; expect a bit of extra scene around every frame.
  • The 3-foot wall: anything closer than about an arm's length plus a hand goes soft. Measure 3 ft against your own body (fingertip to opposite shoulder is close for most people) — that's your built-in ruler forever.
  • Averaged exposure: the camera meters the whole scene. A frame dominated by bright sky comes out dark; a frame dominated by shadow comes out bright. You "set exposure" by choosing how much bright or dark stuff fills the frame — this trick powers silhouettes in Module 1.
🔧 CS-Pro reality check Reviewers consistently describe the CS-Pro's output as deliberately imperfect: visible grain even in daylight, some color fringing on backlit edges, soft corners, punchy color. This curriculum treats those traits as a style — like shooting a good disposable film camera — not as flaws to overcome.

Exercise 0.1 — Calibration roll

⏱ 60–90 mincamera only
  1. Shoot the same simple scene (your yard, a street corner) 4 times — once on each dial position. Log the frame numbers.
  2. Shoot one subject at 5 distances: 1 ft, 2 ft, 3 ft, 6 ft, 15 ft. (Only expect sharpness from 3 ft out.)
  3. Shoot a scene that's mostly sky, then recompose the same scene mostly ground.
  4. Frame a doorway precisely in the viewfinder, edge-to-edge, from about 4 ft away.
  5. Download everything and study: How do the four looks differ? Where does sharpness begin? How did sky vs. ground shift exposure? How much more did the lens see than your finder?

Assignment 0 — 24 frames, no review

⏱ one outingcamera only

Pretend the camera holds one roll of film: exactly 24 exposures on a single walk. Every press must be deliberate — say out loud why before each frame. Review at home in one sitting and pick your best 3. This "one roll" ritual repeats throughout the plan; it's the discipline that makes a screen-free camera a teacher rather than a toy.

MODULE 1

Seeing light: the only exposure control you have

~1–2 weeks · outdoors at varied hours · gear: camera only

With auto-everything, light is your aperture, your shutter dial, and your color grade all at once. Photographers describe light along three axes — learn to name all three before every frame:

  • Quality: hard (small source — direct sun, xenon flash) makes crisp shadows and drama; soft (big source — overcast sky, open shade) makes gentle gradients and rich color.
  • Direction: front light flattens; side light sculpts texture; backlight makes silhouettes, glow, and rim-light.
  • Color: warm at golden hour, blue at dusk, green under trees, mixed at night. The Vintage looks amplify or fight these — a warm look at golden hour doubles down; a cool look at dusk goes moody.

The golden and blue hours

The hour after sunrise and before sunset gives warm, low-angle, low-contrast light — the CS-Pro's sweet spot. The twenty minutes after sunset (blue hour) is dimmer; expect the shutter to drop toward 1/30 s, so brace your elbows or rest the camera on something solid. Grain rises in dim light — treat it as mood.

Backlight and silhouettes: exposure hacking

Because the meter averages the frame, you can force a silhouette: fill most of the frame with bright sky behind a strong shape. The camera exposes for the sky and the subject collapses to black. Reverse it — fill the frame with the shaded side — and the sky blows out to white instead. This is the closest thing you have to exposure compensation; own it.

Exercise 1.1 — One subject, four lights

⏱ across 2–3 dayscamera only

Pick one stationary outdoor subject (a bench, a mailbox, a tree). Photograph it identically composed in: (1) midday sun, (2) overcast/open shade, (3) golden hour side-light, (4) backlit. Same spot, same framing. Compare — this is the fastest way to feel that light, not subject, makes the picture.

Exercise 1.2 — Silhouette hunt

⏱ 60 min at sunsetcamera only

Make 10 silhouettes of objects with strong outlines — fences, bikes, branches, machinery. Rule: the shape must read instantly even as pure black. Keep shapes from overlapping (wide lens tip: step sideways to separate them against the sky).

Assignment 1 — "The same walk, twice"

⏱ 2 outingscamera only

Walk one route at midday, shooting 12 frames. Repeat the identical route and framings at golden hour. Build a side-by-side of the pairs and write one sentence per pair on what the light changed: shadow direction, color, mood, texture.

MODULE 2

Composition: arranging the frame

~1–2 weeks · anywhere · gear: camera only

You can't zoom and you can't blur backgrounds away — on the CS-Pro, composition is done entirely with your feet and your patience. This module drills the six devices that do the most work in landscape, street, abstract, and still-life photography.

1 · Thirds — and when to break them

Place horizons and key subjects on imaginary third-lines rather than dead center. Since your viewfinder has no gridlines, visualize the grid — after a week it becomes automatic. Break the rule deliberately for symmetry (reflections, architecture) by centering with precision.

2 · Leading lines

Roads, rails, fences, shorelines, shadows — lines drag the eye through the frame. The 22.5 mm lens exaggerates convergence dramatically: stand on the line, point it toward your subject, and the wide angle does the rest.

3 · Frames within frames

Shoot through arches, windows, branches, and doorways. A frame-in-frame adds depth, hides messy edges, and — usefully on a wide lens — crops the scene without zooming.

4 · Negative space

Empty sky, water, sand, or wall gives one small subject enormous weight. The wide lens is a negative-space machine — it swallows huge amounts of emptiness. Minimalism also photographs beautifully in flat, overcast light when nothing else does.

5 · Layers

Strong wide-angle photos usually contain a foreground (3–8 ft), midground, and background. Before pressing, ask: "what's my near thing?" If the answer is "nothing," take two steps forward until something enters the bottom of the frame.

6 · Pattern, geometry, and the broken pattern

Repetition (windows, fences, shadows) is instantly graphic; a single interruption in the repetition becomes the subject. Look up at facades and down at pavement.

Exercise 2.1 — One device per outing

⏱ 6 × 30 mincamera only

Six mini-walks, each devoted to a single device above: 8 frames of only leading lines, then 8 of only frames-in-frames, and so on. Constraint breeds seeing.

Exercise 2.2 — Work the scene

⏱ 45 mincamera only

Choose one ordinary subject. Make 12 genuinely different compositions of it without leaving a 30-foot circle: crouch, climb, tilt, center it, third it, frame it, silhouette it, put it tiny in negative space. The tenth idea is where photography starts.

Assignment 2 — The six-frame essay

⏱ one outing + editcamera only

Produce a set of six pictures of one location, using at least four different compositional devices, that hang together as a sequence. Order matters: wide establishing frame → details → a closing image.

MODULE 3

Mastering the wide fixed lens

~1 week · anywhere · gear: camera only

22.5 mm-equivalent is wider than most phone main cameras. Wide lenses don't just "fit more in" — they change spatial relationships: near things get huge, far things shrink, and empty middle distance turns into dead space. The classic beginner failure with this lens is standing too far back and getting a small, cluttered everything-photo. The fix is a mantra:

🥾 The wide-lens mantra "Get close, then get closer — then find the background." Compose in two acts: walk toward your subject until it dominates the frame (stopping at the 3-ft sharpness wall), then move your feet sideways/up/down to place a clean, meaningful background behind it. Foreground first, background second, press third.

Techniques specific to this camera

  • Depth is free. The tiny sensor keeps almost everything sharp from 3 ft to the horizon — so deep near-far compositions (boulder at 3 ft + mountain at 3 miles, both sharp) are your superpower. Blur is not on the menu; depth is.
  • Low and high angles pay double. Wide + low makes foreground textures rush toward the viewer; wide + high flattens scenes into maps. Because there's no screen to squint at, just place the camera at boot level and trust the wide field to catch it.
  • Tilt with intent. Wide lenses make converging verticals obvious. Either hold the camera dead level (buildings stay straight) or tilt hard so the convergence reads as deliberate drama. Halfway looks like a mistake.
  • Shoot blind, on purpose. No screen means no shame: hold the camera overhead in a crowd, at your hip, out of a (stopped!) canoe. The wide angle is forgiving of aim, and blind angles produce frames your eye would never compose. Take three attempts per idea.
  • The finder lies a little. Remember Module 0: the lens sees more than the finder shows. Compose slightly tighter than feels right, and check edges of important frames by taking one "insurance" frame a step closer.

Exercise 3.1 — Three-foot portraits of things

⏱ 45 mincamera only

Photograph 10 objects from exactly your minimum sharp distance (~3 ft), each with a deliberately chosen background. Objects, not people: hydrants, hand tools, flowers, boots. Learn what "close" means on this lens — it's closer than you think.

Exercise 3.2 — Boot-level / sky-level

⏱ 45 mincamera only

20 frames, none taken from eye level. Ten from below knee height, ten from arms fully overhead. Review and note which surprised you — blind shooting with a wide lens is a repeatable creative engine, not a gimmick.

Assignment 3 — Foreground or it didn't happen

⏱ one outingcamera only

A full 24-frame "roll" in which every frame must contain something 3–8 ft from the lens. No exceptions, even for grand vistas. This single constraint fixes 80% of wide-angle photography.

Part II — Subject Studios

Four genres, each shaped around what the CS-Pro does best. Take them in any order — let the season and your week decide.

MODULE 4

Landscape & nature

~2 weeks · trails, parks, water · gear: camera; optional black mist for haze mood

The CS-Pro was practically designed for this genre — pocketable, weatherable-ish, always ready, wide. But small-sensor landscape has one hard rule: the sensor can't hold a bright sky and a dark land at the same time. Great CS-Pro landscapes are therefore made at the edges of the day, in weather, or by choosing which half of the scene to sacrifice.

Field craft

  • Shoot the light, not the view. A mediocre hill in golden side-light beats a famous vista at noon. Plan outings around the first/last 90 minutes of sun.
  • Sky or land — commit. Mostly-sky framing gives you rich clouds and dark, moody land (drama). Mostly-land framing gives you detailed terrain and a blown, white sky (use that white as negative space). Both are valid choices; the 50/50 split is the only failure.
  • Foreground anchor, always (Module 3): rocks, flowers, driftwood, ice, boot prints in the bottom third.
  • Weather is a subject. Fog, drizzle, and storm-light compress contrast into exactly the range the sensor loves — an overcast "bad" day is a gift. (Your mist filters push this further; see Module 8.)
  • Water at 1/30 s. In dim light the shutter floor is 1/30 s — brace the camera on a rock and moving water picks up a whisper of blur while stones stay sharp. It's subtle, but it's the only motion-blur the camera will give you; hunt for it at dusk near streams.
  • Intimate landscapes. When the grand scene isn't working, drop to the small one: a single fern against dark woods, patterns in sand, one glowing backlit leaf. These need only a patch of good light, not a good sky.

Exercise 4.1 — Sky-or-land drill

⏱ 60 min, golden hourcamera only

At one viewpoint, shoot every scene twice: once favoring sky (horizon low), once favoring land (horizon high). At home, pick the stronger of each pair and articulate why. You're training the commit-don't-split instinct.

Exercise 4.2 — The intimate landscape

⏱ 60 min, overcast daycamera only

12 frames, none containing sky or horizon. Forest floor, water edges, rock faces, backlit foliage. Overcast light is your softbox — colors will be deeper than on any sunny day.

Assignment 4 — One place, three moods

⏱ 3 short outingscamera + looks dial

Photograph the same natural location in three different conditions (e.g., morning fog, harsh noon, golden evening), matching the dial look to each mood (cool Vintage for fog, B&W for harsh noon graphics, warm Vintage for evening). Deliver 3 images per mood — nine frames that feel like three different worlds.

MODULE 5

Street & urban

~2 weeks · downtown, markets, transit · gear: camera; optional black mist for neon nights

A small, silent-able, screen-free camera is a street photographer's dream: nobody takes it seriously, and you're never staring at a screen instead of the street. With fixed focus and zero shutter lag excuses, the CS-Pro works like the classic zone-focused film compacts — see, raise, press.

Field craft

  • Fish, don't hunt. Find a good stage — strong light, clean background, a shaft of sun between buildings — and wait for an actor to walk into it. This beats chasing moments, and it's how the wide lens (which needs you close) stays candid: you're already in position, minding your own business.
  • The 6–12 ft zone. At 22.5 mm, street subjects read well at 6–12 ft. Farther and they're specks; remember everything past 3 ft is sharp, so there's no focus excuse for hanging back except nerve.
  • Shoot the city itself. Non-portrait street is rich: signage, storefronts, shadows of pedestrians rather than pedestrians, gestures cropped at the neck down, bikes, queues of objects, reflections in shop glass.
  • Hip shooting (Module 3) shines here — camera at waist level, tilted slightly up, three frames per scene. Review at home like opening presents.
  • Night + xenon = the "party flash" look. The hard, fast xenon flash freezes foreground subjects against dark or dim backgrounds — the gritty direct-flash aesthetic of '90s compacts. Keep subjects 4–8 ft away (closer overexposes, per reviewers), prefer auto-flash over forced, and hunt for reflective surfaces: wet streets, chrome, signage.
  • Vintage looks love cities. The warm look flatters brick and evening bustle; the cool look suits glass, concrete, and rain; B&W strips distracting colored clutter from busy scenes.
⚖️ Courtesy & law Photographing people in public is generally legal in the US, but non-portrait street doesn't need faces at all: shadows, silhouettes, crowds as texture, and the built city are richer subjects for this curriculum. If someone objects, smile, thank them, move on.

Exercise 5.1 — The stage

⏱ 60 mincamera only

Find one patch of dramatic urban light (a sunbeam between buildings, a glowing storefront). Stay 30 minutes. Shoot 15 frames as different elements enter: a bicycle, a dog, a shadow, a pigeon. One stage, many plays.

Exercise 5.2 — City alphabet

⏱ 60–90 mincamera only

Collect 12 urban graphics: signage fragments, painted arrows, hydrants, peeling posters, manhole covers, shadows on brick. Fill the frame (3-ft rule!). Switch to B&W on the dial for half — notice which subjects survive without color.

Assignment 5 — Flash after dark

⏱ one evening outingcamera + flash (auto)

An evening "roll" of 24 frames using flash deliberately: storefront still lifes, wet pavement, bikes, textures at 4–8 ft. Study which distances the xenon exposes well — you're building a personal flash range table no manual can give you.

MODULE 6

Abstract & detail

~1–2 weeks · everywhere, esp. after rain · gear: camera; red filter cameo

Abstraction means photographing light, shape, texture, and color as the subject itself. It's the most forgiving genre for a fixed-focus camera — no decisive moment, no sharpness anxiety — and the most demanding of your eye. Remember the one physical limit: nothing sharp closer than ~3 ft, so "detail" on the CS-Pro means medium-scale texture (a door's worth, not a coin's worth).

Four abstract engines

  • Texture + raking light. Side-light at a shallow angle turns peeling paint, bark, rust, and stone into relief maps. The hour before sunset raking across a wall is a texture factory.
  • Shadow play. Photograph the shadow, not the thing: railings on stairs, leaves on pavement, your own bike's shadow. Hard midday sun — bad for landscapes — is prime time here. The B&W dial position removes color's distraction and doubles the graphic punch.
  • Reflections. Puddles, glass, chrome, wet asphalt. Flip the world: get the camera inches above the puddle (the reflection is optically far away, so the 3-ft rule doesn't apply to it — the mirrored scene stays sharp).
  • Deliberate imperfection. At dusk the 1/30 s floor lets you pan with passing cars or sweep the camera through leaves for painterly smears. Misuse the camera on purpose; review at home; keep the accidents that sing.

Exercise 6.1 — Ten textures, one hour of sun

⏱ 60 min, low suncamera only

Ten surfaces in raking light, frame filled, camera square to the surface (tilt = distortion on a wide lens; here you want flatness). Shoot each in color and B&W. Which textures need color? Which are stronger without it?

Exercise 6.2 — Shadows only

⏱ 45 min, middaycamera, B&W dial

12 frames where the shadow is the subject and its owner is excluded or incidental. Bonus: thread on the red filter with the B&W look — it darkens the blue-lit shadows relative to sunlit areas, making the graphic contrast even harder (a preview of Module 9).

Assignment 6 — "Unrecognizable"

⏱ one outing + editcamera only

Make 8 photographs where a viewer can't immediately name the subject — through crops, reflections, shadows, or blur — but each one has a clear graphic idea (line, shape, rhythm, color block). Show a friend; if they say "what is it?" and keep looking, you've succeeded.

MODULE 7

Still life & found objects

~1 week · home, table by a window, garage sales · gear: camera; white mist cameo

Still life is the genre where you finally control everything — subject, background, and light — which makes it the perfect laboratory for the skills from Modules 1–2. But the CS-Pro imposes two honest constraints: you must stay ≥ 3 ft away, and the wide lens will include lots of surroundings. So think less "macro tabletop" and more environmental still life: the scene around the object is part of the picture. Design it.

Building a CS-Pro still life

  1. Find north-window light. A big window with no direct sun is a giant softbox. Put the table beside it; light rakes across from one side.
  2. Go bigger than you think. Arrangements should span 1–3 ft (jugs, boots, tools, plants, a whole breakfast — not a single ring). Small objects: cluster them.
  3. Work the angles. Straight-down "flat lay" from 3–4 ft above the table works beautifully with a wide lens and sidesteps background problems entirely. For eye-level setups, hang or drape a plain cloth 2–3 ft behind the arrangement.
  4. One hero, supporting cast. Give the arrangement a clear subject, an odd number of elements, and a triangle or diagonal flow. Steal shamelessly from Dutch paintings.
  5. Dial the mood: warm Vintage for kitchen/harvest scenes, B&W for chiaroscuro (one window, dark room, black cloth), cool Vintage for glass and metal. A white mist filter over window light adds a soft period-photograph bloom — see Module 8.
💡 The found still life Half this genre lives outdoors: tools left on a workbench, firewood stacks, garden hoses, tackle boxes, camp kitchens, market produce. "Found" arrangements photographed respectfully (move nothing, or move one thing) train the same eye with zero setup — and suit the camera's grab-and-go spirit.

Exercise 7.1 — One object, five setups

⏱ 60 min indoorscamera + window

Choose one photogenic object (kettle, boot, lantern). Shoot it: (1) window-side light, (2) backlit against the window, (3) flat lay from above, (4) B&W chiaroscuro with dark cloth, (5) with flash, on purpose. Five different pictures of one thing = a masterclass in light direction.

Exercise 7.2 — Found still life walk

⏱ 45 mincamera only

10 found arrangements, moving at most one element per scene. Garages, porches, market stalls, camp sites. Caption each mentally with a story ("someone just left").

Assignment 7 — The seasonal table

⏱ 2 sessionscamera + window + cloth

Design and photograph one deliberate still life that says "this season" (summer fruit, trail gear, harvest, rain jackets and thermos) — minimum 20 frames exploring angle, distance, and dial looks; deliver your best 2. Iteration, not inspiration, is the lesson.

MODULE 8

Mist & diffusion: white vs. black

~1–2 weeks · backlit scenes, evenings, window light · gear: camera + both mist filters

Diffusion filters have microscopic particles in the glass that catch light and spread it: highlights bloom into halos, hard edges soften, and digital crispness melts into something filmic. On a camera that already leans nostalgic, mist filters push the look from "retro digicam" to "found footage from a dream." The two types behave differently, and knowing when to reach for which is the entire skill:

⚪ White mist⚫ Black mist
HighlightsStrong, wide, dreamy halationGentler, tighter glow around lights
Shadows & contrastLifts shadows too — overall contrast drops, image goes airy/foggyKeeps blacks black — contrast largely survives
Overall strengthLoud. The effect is the pictureSubtle. Viewers feel it more than see it
Best CS-Pro subjectsBacklit nature, window-light still life, hazy golden-hour scenes, high-key minimalismNeon & city lights at dusk, sun stars through trees, everyday scenes needing a filmic edge
Worst caseFlat gray mush on overcast days (no highlights to bloom, shadows lifted anyway)Nearly invisible in soft frontal light — save it for scenes with light sources
📷 CS-Pro specifics Mist filters need light sources or strong highlights in (or just outside) the frame to do anything. The sun through leaves, a bright window, neon, lamps, sparkle on water — that's the fuel. Two camera notes: the optical viewfinder won't show you the effect (trust the principles, verify at download), and the light loss is negligible, so mist filters work at any hour — unlike your red filter.

Pairing filters with the looks dial

  • White mist + warm Vintage: maximum nostalgia — summer-memory, home-movie light. Ideal for golden hour nature and still life.
  • Black mist + cool Vintage: the "cinematic" recipe — halated practicals, intact shadows, moody color. Ideal for dusk streets and neon.
  • Black mist + B&W: classic silver-screen halation, like old movie stills.
  • White mist + B&W: pictorialist, early-1900s softness — beautiful for minimal, high-key scenes; too soupy for detailed ones.

Exercise 8.1 — The A/B/C ritual

⏱ 60 min, sunny eveningcamera + both mists

Five backlit or light-source scenes. Shoot each three times from a braced, identical position: bare lens, black mist, white mist (log the order!). Build side-by-side triptychs at home. This one session teaches you more about your specific filters than any article — densities vary by brand, and yours are the only ones that matter.

Exercise 8.2 — Lights at dusk

⏱ 60 min, blue hourcamera + black mist

Shoot storefronts, string lights, headlight streams, and neon with the black mist. Vary your angle to the lights (facing them = big halos; lights at frame edge = gentle wash). Brace against poles — the shutter will be at its 1/30 s floor.

Assignment 8 — One story, one mist

⏱ one outing + editcamera + one mist (your choice)

Choose the filter that suits your subject and shoot a 6-image sequence with a consistent dreamy or cinematic mood: "morning at the lake," "main street at dusk." The test of success: the six images feel like stills from the same film.

MODULE 9

The red filter: dramatic black & white

~1–2 weeks · bright sunny days with clouds · gear: camera + red filter, B&W dial

A red filter passes red light and blocks blue and green. In black & white, that means: blue skies go nearly black, white clouds detonate against them, atmospheric haze vanishes, foliage darkens, and red/orange subjects (brick, rust, canyon rock) glow bright. It's the classic recipe behind dramatic mid-century landscape photography — and your CS-Pro's B&W dial position turns it into a point-and-shoot superpower.

How tones translate

Real-world colorThrough red filter (in B&W)Creative consequence
Blue skyVery dark gray → near blackClouds pop; skies gain weight and menace
White clouds / snowBright, fully detailedMaximum cloud drama; wait for cumulus days
Green foliage / grassDarker than expectedForests go somber; backlit leaves still glow
Red brick, rust, canyon rockLight, luminousRed subjects separate beautifully from sky and foliage
Haze / distant atmosphereCut dramaticallyDistant ridgelines sharpen; long views regain contrast
Blue waterDark, inkyLakes and sea turn dramatic; sparkle highlights stand out
📷 CS-Pro specifics — read before your first outing
  • A red filter eats ~3 stops of light. The auto-exposure will compensate by slowing the shutter and raising gain — fine in full sun, grainy and shake-prone in shade or evening. Rule: red filter = bright daylight only. The "bad" harsh midday light other modules avoid is exactly when this filter works best.
  • Set the dial to B&W first, then thread the filter. Shot in color through a red filter, everything is just… red. (Shoot one on purpose — the monochrome-red look is its own experiment.)
  • Your viewfinder stays clear. Because it's not through-the-lens, you compose normally while the lens sees deep red. Film shooters never had it so easy.
  • Custom B&W looks multiply this. Module 10 covers loading a high-contrast .flt (e.g., an FP4/HP5-inspired curve) into the B&W slot — filter + curve is the full film-landscape recipe.

Learning to see in monochrome

B&W photography is about tonal contrast, not color contrast. A red flower on green leaves is vivid in color but — through a red filter — becomes light-on-dark drama; a blue door on gray wall almost disappears. Before each frame ask: "what's my brightest tone, what's my darkest, and do they touch?" If the answer is "everything's middle gray," walk on.

Exercise 9.1 — Filter on, filter off

⏱ 45 min, sunny + cloudscamera + red filter, B&W dial

Eight scenes with sky in them, each shot twice: B&W bare lens, then B&W + red filter, identical framing. The pairs will teach you exactly what your filter's strength does to sky, cloud, skin of buildings, and foliage.

Exercise 9.2 — Red renaissance

⏱ 60 mincamera + red filter

Hunt subjects that a red filter lightens: brick walls, rusted metal, red barns, terracotta, autumn leaves. Compose them against darkened skies or foliage — you're painting with the filter's tonal flip.

Assignment 9 — The dramatic land

⏱ one bright outing + editcamera + red filter

A 24-frame B&W landscape "roll" on a day with real clouds: big skies, strong foregrounds (Module 3), tonal thinking throughout. Deliver 4 finals. Optional wildcard: stack black mist + red for two frames — halated highlights over inky skies is a look almost nobody has seen from a $99 camera.

MODULE 10

Digital film looks: mastering the .flt system

~1–2 weeks · desk + field testing · gear: camera, USB-C cable, computer

Every position on the CS-Pro's dial — Standard, Vintage 1, Vintage 2, B&W — is just the camera reading a small file from its memory: std.flt, vtg1.flt, vtg2.flt, or bw.flt. A .flt file is a plain-text recipe of tone and color transformations — essentially a lookup table (LUT) — that the firmware loads at power-on and bakes into every JPEG as you shoot. Replace the file, restart the camera, and that dial position becomes a different film stock. This is the closest thing the CS-Pro has to interchangeable film — and it turns a four-look camera into an infinite-look camera.

What a .flt can and cannot do

✅ A .flt controls❌ A .flt cannot change
Tone curve — brightness, contrast, lifted "faded film" blacks, rolled-off highlightsGrain — the sensor's native grain is always there (part of the charm)
Color balance — overall shifts and split-tone effects (e.g., warm highlights, teal shadows) via per-channel valuesSharpness — fixed by the lens and processor
Saturation — from oversaturated slide-film pop to muted pastels to full monochromeSelective hues — you can't target "only the greens" like a Lightroom HSL panel; shifts are global

Think of it as choosing a film stock, not editing a photo: global curves plus color character. That constraint is why well-made .flt looks feel like film rather than like a filter app.

⚠️ Before you flash your first filter
  • Looks are baked in at capture. There's no RAW file — a photo taken with a bad filter is permanently that photo. Always test a new look on one throwaway "roll" before an outing that matters.
  • Filters are model-specific. A .flt made for the original V103b (e.g., Milunich Photography's excellent free library of 30+ film emulations) is not guaranteed to work on a CS-Pro. Prefer files labeled CS-Pro, or make your own with the official tool.
  • Back up the factory looks first. Before overwriting anything, copy any .flt files you find on the camera to a folder on your computer — and know that the official filters page hosts the defaults if you ever need to restore.
  • Naming is strict: lowercase, exact — std.flt, vtg1.flt, vtg2.flt, bw.flt — in the camera's root directory. Restart the camera after copying; on boot the camera confirms a custom filter has loaded.

Installing and managing a look library

  1. On your computer, keep a library folder of filters under their real names (portra-soft.flt, fp4-highcon.flt…), with a note about each look. Only rename copies to the slot names.
  2. Connect the camera by USB-C; it mounts as a drive. Copy the renamed file to the root directory, replacing the slot you're changing.
  3. Eject, restart the camera, and confirm the custom look loaded.
  4. Re-shoot your Module 0 calibration scene (same spot, all four dial positions) after every change. Over time this gives you an honest catalog of every look you've ever run — the screen-free camera's version of film-stock test rolls.

Designing looks that work — a starter theory

  • Lift the black point → "faded film." Raising shadows-output slightly is the single most film-like move. Pair with a touch of warmth in the shadows for a sun-aged print feel.
  • Roll off the highlights → protect skies. A gentle shoulder at the top of the curve tames the CS-Pro's tendency to clip bright skies — the closest you'll get to "dynamic range" on this camera.
  • Split-tone for cinema. Warm highlights + cool shadows = the teal-orange look; cool highlights + warm shadows = old postcard. Keep shifts small — they're applied to every pixel of every photo.
  • Desaturate before you stylize. Pulling saturation 10–20% makes almost any curve look more like film and less like an app filter.
  • Design for a purpose, not a preview. A look that flatters one test photo may fail in other light. The great ones are designed for a situation: "golden-hour trails," "overcast city," "red-filter skies."

Suggested CS-Pro loadout for this curriculum: keep std.flt factory (your control); load a warm, faded film emulation in vtg1.flt (landscape, still life, white-mist pairing); a cool cinematic look in vtg2.flt (street, dusk, black-mist pairing); and a high-contrast Ilford-style curve in bw.flt tuned for red-filter landscapes (Module 9).

Exercise 10.1 — Borrow before you build

⏱ 60 min desk + 45 min fieldcamera + USB-C
  1. Back up the camera's current .flt files to your library folder.
  2. Download two ready-made CS-Pro looks (e.g., Kino Control from Camp Shades and one film emulation) and install them in the two Vintage slots.
  3. Shoot your calibration scene plus a 12-frame mini-roll alternating dial positions on identical scenes.
  4. Review: describe each look in one sentence ("lifted blacks, green shadows, tame highlights"). Reverse-engineering vocabulary is the foundation for designing your own.

Exercise 10.2 — Design "Faded Summer," your first original

⏱ 90 mincomputer + camera

Using the official designer (upload one of your own Module 4 photos as the preview) or the ramp method, build a look to this recipe: black point lifted slightly, highlights rolled off, a touch of warmth overall, saturation −15%. Install it as vtg1.flt, shoot a golden-hour mini-roll, then iterate: adjust the recipe once based on real results and reflash. Design → shoot → revise is the whole craft.

Exercise 10.3 — The red-filter B&W curve

⏱ 60 min + one sunny outingcamera + red filter + computer

Build a punchy monochrome for bw.flt: strong S-curve (deep blacks, bright detailed whites), zero saturation. Then repeat Exercise 9.1's filter-on/filter-off pairs with the new curve. Factory B&W + red filter was good; a curve designed for the red filter's flattened blue channel is the full Ansel-style recipe from a $99 camera.

Assignment 10 — Your signature stock

⏱ 2 weeks, ongoingeverything

Commit to one self-designed look as your only color look for two full weeks across at least three modules' subjects. Keep a change log (v1, v2, v3…) as you refine it between outings. At the end, name it. Photographers spend years finding "their" film stock — you get to author yours.

Self-critique: the five questions

For each keeper, answer honestly:

  • What is the subject — can I name it in three words?
  • What does the light do for this picture that flat light wouldn't?
  • What compositional device carries it, and is there a foreground?
  • What would I exclude if I could reshoot?
  • Would this survive as one of only 24 frames?

Rotate your film stocks

Module 10 made the dial programmable — keep it alive. A sustainable rhythm: revisit your look loadout once a season (new light, new palette), keep your library folder and change log current, and re-shoot the calibration scene after every reflash. One custom look per season, refined across many rolls, beats ten downloaded ones.

Capstone projects (pick one per season)

📕 The Zine

Twenty final images from one theme ("Water," "Rust," "My Street at Dusk") sequenced into a small printed zine or PDF. Sequencing is a composition skill of its own.

🗓️ 30 Days, 30 Frames

One — exactly one — exposure per day for a month. The ultimate screen-free discipline; the month's contact sheet becomes a diary.

🧭 One Mile Radius

Everything within a mile of home, across all four subject modules, until strangers wouldn't believe where it was shot. Constraint of place = depth of seeing.

🎞️ Three Filters, One Scene

A single location shot bare, white mist, black mist, and red+B&W across matched conditions — presented as a study of what glass alone can do.
